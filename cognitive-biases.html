25 Cognitive Biases That Trick Your Brain Every Day

Last updated: June 2026 | 20 min read | By Itty Bitty Bites

Your brain is not a rational machine. It is a pattern-matching engine that takes shortcuts to save energy. These shortcuts are called cognitive biases, and they affect every decision you make -- from what you buy to who you trust to how you vote. Here are 25 of the most common ones, with real-world examples you will recognize from your own life.

Decision-Making Biases

1. Confirmation Bias

You seek out and remember information that confirms what you already believe, while ignoring evidence that contradicts it.

Example: After deciding to buy a specific car, you suddenly notice that car everywhere and read only positive reviews.

2. Anchoring Bias

The first piece of information you encounter disproportionately influences your judgment, even if it is irrelevant.

Example: A store marks a shirt as "Was $120, now $40." You feel like you are getting a deal, even if the shirt was never worth $120.

3. Availability Heuristic

You judge the probability of events based on how easily examples come to mind, not on actual statistics.

Example: After seeing news about a plane crash, you feel flying is dangerous, even though you are 100x more likely to die in a car accident.

4. Sunk Cost Fallacy

You continue investing in something because of what you have already spent, rather than evaluating future value.

Example: Sitting through a terrible movie because "I already paid for the ticket."

5. Loss Aversion

Losing something feels roughly twice as painful as gaining the same thing feels good.

Example: You feel worse about losing $50 than you feel good about finding $50.

6. Framing Effect

Your decision changes based on how information is presented, even when the facts are identical.

Example: "90% fat-free" sounds healthy. "Contains 10% fat" sounds unhealthy. Same product.

7. Decoy Effect

Adding a third, inferior option makes one of the original two look much more attractive.

Example: Small popcorn $4, Large $8. Nobody buys large. Add Medium $7.50 -- suddenly Large looks like a great deal.

Social Biases

8. Dunning-Kruger Effect

People with low ability overestimate their competence, while experts underestimate theirs.

Example: Someone who took one coding tutorial thinks they can build Facebook. A senior engineer with 20 years of experience says "I still have so much to learn."

9. Halo Effect

Your overall impression of a person influences how you judge their specific traits.

Example: An attractive person is assumed to be smart, kind, and competent -- with no evidence for any of those traits.

10. Bandwagon Effect

You adopt beliefs or behaviors because many other people do, regardless of evidence.

Example: A restaurant with a long line seems better than an empty one, even if the food is identical.

11. Authority Bias

You give more weight to opinions from authority figures, even when they speak outside their expertise.

Example: A famous actor endorsing a health supplement. They know acting, not biochemistry.

12. In-Group Bias

You favor people who belong to your group over outsiders, even when groups are arbitrary.

Example: Studies show that even dividing people by coin flip creates bias toward "your" group within minutes.

13. Bystander Effect

The more people present during an emergency, the less likely any individual is to help.

Example: Someone collapses in a crowded mall. Everyone assumes someone else will call 911.

Memory Biases

14. Hindsight Bias

After learning the outcome, you believe you "knew it all along."

Example: After a team loses, fans say "I knew they would lose" -- but they did not predict it beforehand.

15. False Memory

You confidently remember events that never happened or remember them differently from how they occurred.

Example: Many people "remember" seeing news footage of the first plane hitting the Tower on 9/11. No such footage aired live.

16. Recency Bias

You give more weight to recent events than to older ones when making judgments.

Example: A stock that went up this week feels like a good investment, ignoring its poor 5-year performance.

17. Peak-End Rule

You judge experiences based on how they felt at their most intense moment and at the end, not on the average.

Example: A vacation with one amazing day and a smooth last day feels great, even if most days were mediocre.

Perception Biases

18. Negativity Bias

Negative experiences have a greater impact on your mind than positive ones of equal intensity.

Example: One negative comment on social media affects you more than 100 positive ones.

19. Spotlight Effect

You overestimate how much other people notice your appearance and behavior.

Example: You spill coffee on your shirt and feel like everyone is staring. In reality, almost nobody noticed.

20. Illusory Truth Effect

Hearing a statement repeated makes you more likely to believe it is true, regardless of its accuracy.

Example: Advertising slogans repeated thousands of times become "facts" in your mind.

21. Optimism Bias

You overestimate the likelihood of positive events happening to you and underestimate negative ones.

Example: Smokers know smoking causes cancer but believe it will not happen to them specifically.

Reasoning Biases

22. Survivorship Bias

You focus on successes and ignore failures, giving you a distorted view of reality.

Example: "College dropouts like Bill Gates became billionaires, so dropping out is fine." You do not hear about the millions who dropped out and struggled.

23. Gambler's Fallacy

You believe past random events affect future probabilities.

Example: A coin landed heads 5 times in a row. "Tails is DUE." Each flip is still exactly 50/50.

24. Fundamental Attribution Error

You attribute others' behavior to their character but your own behavior to circumstances.

Example: Someone cuts you off in traffic -- "What a jerk." You cut someone off -- "I was late for an important meeting."

25. Status Quo Bias

You prefer the current state of affairs and resist change, even when change would benefit you.

Example: Staying with an expensive phone plan because switching "seems like too much hassle."

How to Fight Cognitive Biases

You cannot eliminate biases -- they are hardwired into your brain. But you can train yourself to recognize them in the moment. Here are three practical steps:

  1. Slow down: Most biases operate through System 1 (fast, automatic thinking). Engaging System 2 (slow, deliberate thinking) catches many errors before they become decisions.
  2. Seek disconfirming evidence: Actively look for reasons you might be wrong. Ask "What would change my mind?"
  3. Practice cognitive training: Games that require you to override automatic responses (like the Stroop Effect) strengthen the neural circuits responsible for catching biases.
