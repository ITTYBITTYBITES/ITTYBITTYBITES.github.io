How to Improve Decision Making: 12 Science-Backed Methods

Last updated: June 2026 | 18 min read | By Itty Bitty Bites

Every day you make approximately 35,000 decisions. Most of them happen automatically -- what to eat, which route to take, whether to trust a stranger. Only a handful get your full conscious attention. The problem? Your automatic decision system (what psychologist Daniel Kahneman calls System 1) is fast but riddled with blind spots. The good news: decision-making is a skill, and skills can be trained.

Here are 12 methods backed by cognitive science research that genuinely improve how you make decisions -- from split-second calls to life-changing choices.

1. Recognize Your Two Decision Systems

Your brain operates two parallel decision systems. System 1 is fast, automatic, and intuitive -- it identifies a face in milliseconds, catches a ball without calculating trajectories, and reads words before you can stop yourself. System 2 is slow, deliberate, and analytical -- it solves math problems, weighs pros and cons, and plans for the future.

Most decision errors happen when System 1 handles a situation that requires System 2. The first step to better decisions is recognizing WHICH system is making the call. When you feel instant certainty about a complex question, that is System 1. Pause and ask: "Is this actually simple, or am I being lazy?"

Action: For the next week, label each major decision "System 1" or "System 2" after you make it. Notice how often you default to automatic mode on important choices.

2. Train Your Rapid Cognition

While System 1 makes errors, it also makes brilliant snap judgments when properly trained. Malcolm Gladwell's research in "Blink" shows that experts can make accurate split-second decisions that outperform lengthy analysis -- but only in their domain of expertise. A firefighter who "feels" a floor is about to collapse, an ER doctor who instantly recognizes sepsis, a chess grandmaster who sees the winning move in seconds -- all are using trained rapid cognition.

The key is deliberate exposure to patterns under time pressure. The more scenarios you process quickly, the more accurate your snap judgments become in that domain.

Action: Practice rapid identification in any domain that matters to you. Brain training games that force responses under time constraints (like The 2-Second Witness) build this skill across 60 knowledge domains.

3. Pre-commit to Decision Rules

Research by psychologist Peter Gollwitzer shows that "implementation intentions" -- if-then rules you create in advance -- dramatically improve decision quality. Instead of deciding in the moment (when emotions, fatigue, and bias are strongest), you decide your policy in advance when thinking clearly.

Examples: "If I receive a suspicious email asking for credentials, I will call IT before clicking any links." "If an investment drops more than 15%, I will sell regardless of how I feel." "If I am offered dessert after 9 PM, I will decline."

Action: Identify your 3 most common bad decisions. Write an if-then rule for each. Post them where you will see them.

4. Practice the 10/10/10 Framework

Developed by Suzy Welch, this simple framework overcomes emotional short-termism. Before making a decision, ask: How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? This forces your brain to shift from System 1's emotional response to System 2's long-term perspective.

This is especially powerful for decisions driven by anger, fear, or impulse. The 10-minute feeling is almost always different from the 10-year impact.

Action: Use 10/10/10 on your next 3 significant decisions. Write down all three timeframe answers before choosing.

5. Seek Disconfirming Evidence

Confirmation bias is the most destructive cognitive bias in decision making. Your brain actively seeks information that supports what you already believe and ignores information that contradicts it. This feels comfortable but leads to terrible decisions.

The antidote is to actively seek reasons you might be wrong. Ask: "What evidence would change my mind?" If you cannot answer that question, you are not thinking -- you are defending.

Action: Before your next important decision, write down 3 reasons you might be wrong. If this feels uncomfortable, you are doing it correctly.

6. Use Probabilistic Thinking

Most people think in binary: something will either happen or it will not. Superforecasters (studied by Philip Tetlock) think in probabilities: "There is a 70% chance this will work." This seemingly small shift dramatically improves decision accuracy because it forces you to consider uncertainty rather than pretending it does not exist.

Assign percentage probabilities to outcomes. Track your accuracy over time. You will discover that some of your "certainties" should be closer to 60/40.

Action: For the next month, assign probability percentages to your predictions. At the end, check your calibration. Were your "90% confident" predictions right 90% of the time?

7. Reduce Decision Fatigue

Your decision quality degrades throughout the day as your mental resources deplete. A famous study of Israeli judges found that prisoners requesting parole were granted freedom 65% of the time in morning sessions but only 10% in late afternoon sessions -- the judges' decision fatigue led them to default to "no."

Protect your best decisions by making them when you are freshest. Automate or eliminate trivial decisions to preserve willpower for what matters.

Action: Make your most important decisions before noon. Automate 3 daily decisions this week (what to wear, what to eat for breakfast, when to exercise).

8. Build Mental Models

Charlie Munger (Warren Buffett's partner) attributes his success to maintaining a "latticework of mental models" -- frameworks from different disciplines that help analyze problems. Instead of seeing every problem through one lens, you apply the most relevant model from your collection.

Key mental models for decision making: inversion (what would make this fail?), second-order thinking (what happens AFTER what happens?), opportunity cost (what am I giving up?), and Occam's razor (the simplest explanation is usually correct).

Action: Learn one new mental model per week. Apply it to a real decision within 48 hours.

9. Learn from Expert Decision Makers

Gary Klein's research on naturalistic decision making reveals how experts in high-stakes fields (firefighters, soldiers, surgeons) make rapid accurate decisions. They do not analyze options -- they recognize patterns from experience and simulate the first plausible option mentally. If the simulation works, they act. This "recognition-primed decision model" explains why experience in a domain is irreplaceable.

The game The 2-Second Witness replicates this process: rapid pattern presentation, time pressure, and immediate feedback across 60 knowledge domains from cybersecurity to medicine to law.

Action: Study how experts in your field make decisions. Interview them. Ask not "what did you decide?" but "what did you notice that told you what to do?"

10. Develop Metacognitive Awareness

Metacognition is "thinking about thinking." Research consistently shows that people who regularly reflect on their decision-making process make better decisions. This is not rumination (replaying past events with regret) -- it is structured review of HOW you decided, not just WHAT you decided.

After important decisions, ask: What information did I use? What information did I ignore? What biases might have been active? Would I make the same decision with different information?

Action: Keep a decision journal. For each major decision, write: what you decided, why, what you expected, and what actually happened. Review monthly.

11. Train Under Time Pressure

Research from the Stroop Effect literature shows that time pressure reveals your true decision-making ability. Without pressure, anyone can appear rational by deliberating endlessly. Under time constraints, your trained instincts and automatic biases emerge.

Regular practice under time pressure builds faster, more accurate automatic responses. Athletes call this "training at game speed." Cognitive scientists call it "building automaticity." Both describe the same phenomenon: repeated exposure under realistic conditions rewires your brain for rapid accuracy.

Action: Practice decision-making tasks with a timer. Set yourself 30-second deadlines for work decisions that normally take 5 minutes. You will discover your first instinct is right more often than you expect -- especially after training.

12. Sleep on Important Decisions

Neuroscience research by Ap Dijksterhuis demonstrates that for complex decisions with many variables, unconscious processing (letting the problem "incubate" during sleep or distraction) often outperforms conscious deliberation. Your sleeping brain continues processing information and can integrate more factors simultaneously than your conscious mind.

This does NOT mean avoiding decisions. It means: gather information thoroughly, sleep on it, then trust the answer your refreshed brain provides in the morning. This works because sleep consolidates relevant memories and prunes irrelevant ones.

Action: For your next complex decision (more than 4 factors), gather all information during the day, sleep on it, and decide in the morning before checking email or social media.
