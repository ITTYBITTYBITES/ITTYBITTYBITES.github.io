15 Logical Fallacies You Commit Every Day (With Examples)

Last updated: June 2026 | By Itty Bitty Bites

A logical fallacy is a flaw in reasoning that makes an argument invalid -- even when the conclusion might be true. You encounter these every day in conversations, news, social media, and advertising. Learning to spot them is one of the most powerful decision-making skills you can develop.

Fallacies of Relevance

1. Ad Hominem (Attack the Person)

Attacking the person making the argument instead of the argument itself. "You can't trust his opinion on climate change -- he failed chemistry in college." His grades are irrelevant to whether the data is correct.

2. Straw Man

Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack. Person A: "We should have stricter gun regulations." Person B: "Why do you want to ban all guns and leave people defenseless?" Person A never said ban ALL guns.

3. Appeal to Authority

Claiming something is true because an authority figure said it -- even if they are outside their expertise. "This physicist says the economy will crash, so it must be true." Physicists are not economists.

4. Red Herring

Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original argument. "Why should we worry about pollution when there are children going hungry?" Both problems can exist and be addressed simultaneously.

5. Appeal to Emotion

Using emotions instead of logic to support an argument. "Think of the children!" is often used to shut down rational analysis of policies that may or may not affect children.

Fallacies of Presumption

6. False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

Presenting only two options when more exist. "You're either with us or against us." Reality usually has many positions between full support and full opposition.

7. Slippery Slope

Arguing that one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without evidence. "If we allow students to use calculators, soon they won't be able to do any math at all." Each step in the chain needs its own evidence.

8. Circular Reasoning

Using the conclusion as a premise. "The Bible is true because it is the word of God, and we know it is the word of God because the Bible says so." The argument assumes what it is trying to prove.

9. Hasty Generalization

Drawing a broad conclusion from a small or unrepresentative sample. "I met two rude people from that city, so everyone there must be rude." Two data points cannot represent an entire population.

10. Begging the Question

Making an argument that requires the conclusion to be true in order for the premise to be true. "Free speech is important because people should be able to say what they want." This restates the conclusion as the reason.

Fallacies of Ambiguity

11. Equivocation

Using a word with multiple meanings and switching between them. "The sign said 'fine for parking here.' Since it is fine, I'll park here." "Fine" means a monetary penalty, not "acceptable."

12. Tu Quoque (You Too)

Deflecting criticism by pointing out the critic does the same thing. "You say I should eat healthier, but you ate pizza last night." The criticism may be valid regardless of the critic's behavior.

13. Bandwagon (Appeal to Popularity)

Arguing something is true or good because many people believe or do it. "Millions of people can't be wrong." Yes, they absolutely can. Millions of people believed the Earth was flat.

14. Gambler's Fallacy

Believing past random events affect future probabilities. "The coin landed heads 5 times in a row, so tails is due." Each flip is independent -- still 50/50.

15. Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing something because of past investment rather than future value. "I've already watched 2 hours of this terrible movie, I might as well finish it." Those 2 hours are gone regardless of whether you stay or leave. Only future enjoyment matters.

How to Use This Knowledge

The goal is not to "win" arguments but to think more clearly. When you catch a fallacy -- in someone else's argument OR your own -- you have identified where reasoning has gone wrong.
